The epic and phenomenal West-African inspired Amazon bestselling fantasy from burgeoning new talents.

The authors would love you to join them on a voyage through ancient Africa by immersing yourself in this riveting story. View the world through the eyes of the enthralling characters as they dance to the hymns played by whatever gods they serve.

King Ewuare is dead; another King has been captured. Now, vultures are circling to devour the once-great kingdom, Mombaka. Across all five kingdoms below the Moon Sea, new alliances are being forged to replace old ones as Kings and Queens vie for power. After betraying and leading her husband to slaughter, Queen Meha and her son, Kendi, return home to collect her prize as she watches the little boy ascend the throne.

Princess Jamila remains in Amarea, navigating her way through an uneasy marriage to the Amarean Crown Prince, Gambo, with only her wits to aid her. Queen Idioba journeys deep into the mythical Agbon forest in search of a cure for the cursed Prince, Kamunde, facing many mysterious creatures on her way.

Foreign merchantmen arrive in Songhai, bringing a new religion accompanied by peace and prosperity on the one hand, and chaos and destruction on the other. Across the Moon Sea, Eweka climbs up the ranks in the Vulkan army, while Lehani travels across treacherous lands and stormy waters to have her revenge on him. Up in the cold mountains and the valleys in-between, the Orbs continue rising, gaining power and strength as the lighting god prepares to unleash the great war upon them all.

About the author

E.O. Odiase

2 books62 followers
E.O. ODIASE IS AN AMAZON BESTSELLING AUTHOR IN AFRICAN LITERATURE.

Growing up, we were taught colonial and post-colonial African history. It is rare to hear of African history, tales, or traditions before slavery. The lack of information pushed me to uncover a broader truth about our my culture. For a few years now, I have visited several museums, showing a variety of ancient artefacts, such as the Benin Bronzes.

My fascination for storytelling and the truth told through it, combined with extensive research, both in-person and online has allowed me to paint a picture of the lives of our West African ancestors and mythology.

Nosa Odiase is a Kings College London graduate, with a degree in Mechanical Engineering. He is a Chartered Engineer and has been working in the industry for over six years.

This books will be released the 16/2/2021.

“A Dance for the Gods” continues a few months after the first installment in “The Last Warrior King” series, “A Cry to War”. We follow a large ensemble of characters. A princess who makes it her mission to kill the man who murdered her brother. A lost soldier and escaped slave trying to create a new life for himself in the hopes of becoming one of the greatest warriors of his time. A rightful king who goes on a quest with campaigns to ask the help of the Gods to lift his curse. A young “temporary” queen who just received a throne she never sought after. A queen who starts to realize her conniving skims is blowing up in her face. A Boy King who rules by listening to the strange voices in his head (Gods? Or is he losing his mind?)

In this world, there are 5 kingdoms. Some have formed alliances while others have made enemies. There is a running internal/external conflict with almost every character regarding the Gods: are they real? If so, why don’t they help their people? Which god is the true God? Are the Gods good or evil? This conflict feels so real as you jump from character to character and think about our world today. A lot of these characters never questioned their god and for the first time, we get to experience with them their thoughts when they start to doubt in their gods (and seek another?).

There is even more political intrigue in this second book as followers of the newfound God, the Elemental God pushes the ideas of the Old Gods away through cunning tactics. People switch sides (willingly and forcefully to survive). Alliances are forged and broken. And the dead Queen has awakened!

There are a lot of different locations that we the readers get to experience. From the forest, desert, to the sea. My favorite being the Agbon Forest. We see a lot more of the Agbon Forest and I LOVED every second of it! I’m a sucker for enchanted forest (scary or not). The forest and the creatures who live in the forest like the Orbs and Moon-Eyed creatures are still a mystery but we now get a feel of its scenery. My second favorite scenery in the desert. But I think that’s because I enjoyed the banter between the Sandstorm brothers and Eweka. You forget how vast and wide Africa is. It’s surprising more authors don’t write fantasy worlds inspired by Africa as this book has and I think does it well but still reaches modern fantasy fans at the same time.

I recommend this book for anyone who enjoys books centered around political intrigue, a fantasy that follows multiple POVs, and for those interested in reading a fantasy novel who is inspired by African culture. Note: This book has explicit sex scenes and violence.

The biggest thing that stopped me from enjoying this book was, this felt like a complete ripoff of a song of ice and fire. The story continues where A Cry to War left off. Eweke was captured and enslaved but escapes to join the Vulkan army. He becomes a warrior and assassin for hire. His position leads him to commit terrible acts which he can barely live with but he takes comfort in Lehani, a slave assigned to his personal service. After killing King Ewuare, Meha and her son Kendi take the throne. Kendi is too young to rule and his childish ways cause havoc as he makes hasty and disastrous decisions for the kingdom. The once-celebrated land is slowly being weakened against its enemies. Meha's lover Kofi is caught in the crossfire and quickly realises she will sacrifice him to save her children.

Throughout the kingdoms, new royalty emerges and the consequences are felt across the lands as friend become foe and sworn enemies make tentative alliances. Meanwhile, the Orbs of the God Ogun are growing stronger and their creatures are on the attack. Whilst the kingdoms below the mountain fight amongst themselves, few humans realise the real threat will come from above.

This was a solid 🌟🌟🌟🌟 read jam-packed with plots of betrayal, violence, crusades and family drama. There was a wealth of plot threads in this story that kept the story interesting. My favourite part was Eweke's journey as his path was varied and full of emotional scenes and traumatic events. I was drawn to this book because of this gorgeous cover, my love and interest in the enamoring African culture, and of course the highly interesting synopsis. I didn't realize until later that it was the second book in the Last Warrior King series (bummer), but thankfully I didn't face a lot of trouble because I had read quite a few reviews of the first book, so I could more or less grasp what was happening.
The book was overall well-planned and thoughtfully laid out, and had a plot-driven storyline. There was a huge cast of characters and pretty much included all the elements that are expected from a high fantasy novel. What really bothered me was the writing style and the fact that the characters felt too shallow and like puppets being controlled by the author (which they are, but they aren't supposed to read like that).

It was kinda hard for me to get into because in the first few chapters there was a LOT of telling instead of showing. And every time the characters did something to actually make you like them or invest in them, they went ahead and did something exceedingly out of character and something that didn't suit them. It just didn't make sense. In fact, those scenes felt like parts of a fan-fiction.
Another thing was that the author has a weird way of referring to the characters. For example, there is a character named Meha, and she is supposed to be a conniving woman. And every single time that he talks about Meha, he says it like the "conniving woman" did this and that. At one point, it got so annoying that I almost screamed - "I get that Meha is a conniving woman. But what's wrong with pronouns?"

Also, some parts of the story didn't feel ORIGINAL. The "Boy King" named Kendi, (who was referred to as the boy king all throughout) had the exact similar traits, storyline, and characteristics as Joffrey (from A Song of Ice and Fire series/ Game of Thrones) except younger.

And one last thing that disturbed me was the sense of "time" in the story. There was no logical explanation as to how much time had passed between one incident and the other. I could make out that a long time had passed, but exactly how much was unclear. And because of this same reason, the transition between two povs was harsh and took a while to get into

The book was good enough. And I must admit the only I kept reading, was because I really admired a few characters (namely two - Eweka and Hasina) and there were pet lions (!!!!). That won me over me honestly.

I am kinda interested to know what happens in the next book.
