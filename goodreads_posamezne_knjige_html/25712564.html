The life of a star-studded royal has not been kind to eighteen year-old Elena Watkins. With a Council breathing down her neck and a dragon that refuses to accept her as her rider, she must convince everyone that she is ready to rule Paegeia like her parents before her. But she has made a promise to her father King Albert, that she will not go looking for him and free the people of Etan. Elena has promised to never truly fulfil her destiny.

However, situations out of her control will soon force her to confront herself and the evil that seeks to destroy her. Elena must look inside herself to discover if she can defeat the approaching darkness, be accepted by the people of Paegeia, bring her dragon back to light and fulfill the destiny written in their stars.

My 2025 reviews of the whole Dragonian series can be found here: Book #1 Firebolt, Book #2 Thunderlight, Book #3 Frostbite, Book #5 Starlight.

Seems I never wrote a review of this installment in the past, nor did I record more readings than just the first time. Strange, I'm sure I read this book several times in like 2016-2018.

Moonbreeze has a lot to unpack. While it's the book that really amps up both the main plot and Elena's relationship with Blake, it also comes out swinging with some more plot points that weren't well thought out—and the heavy, poorly executed inclusion of sexual assault.

But we’ll get to that.

Moonbreeze has some format revamping that makes it differ from the first three installments. The book is fairly long and separated into sections that focus on the various goals of the novel—like Elena trying to get Blake to accept her as his rider/dent, developing their relationship, and making serious progress on the major conflict of “defeat Goron and save Paegia.” Part of the way in, we get sections within chapters that are from Blake's POV instead of Elena's. I think the inclusion of Blake's POV was a very good choice; it gives a lot more depth to the story and really helps flesh it out. I greatly approve of this move for the book and series.

I'll admit, I was pretty engaged with the story. It's been so long since I read it that a lot of details had been lost to time, and I wanted to see what was going to happen next. I've said it before, but the author's passion for her story shines through and there's a quality to this series that is evident despite how much the author's writing drags the books down. As a romance lover, this installment finally starting to really focus in on the development of Elena and Blake’s relationship made it the first read in the series that I really, really liked. Previously, the romance with Lucian was so empty, it didn’t feel like romance.

With the good stuff out of the way, we must now turn to talking about the onslaught of things worth heavy critique. Warning for this section, we have to talk about some sensitive subject matter.

I'll get the elephant in the room taken care of first: the author decided that Elena had to be gang r*ped in this installment. Blake doesn't take part, thankfully, so it's not that kind of yikes. The yikes lies in the fact that Elena being assaulted was not needed, had no real justification, and wasn’t handled with as much sensitivity as it should have been.

The assault, when you get down to it, happens in this book for Blake.

It’s there to punish him for the way he’s behaved towards Elena, to be an obstacle in his relationship with her and to motivate his character growth—it doesn’t really even center Elena’s healing or have anything meaningful to say about sexual assault or the experience of survivors. What makes it all the worse is just how much it wasn’t needed. There was already plenty of validation provided in Moonbreeze and the three proceeding novels for all the relationship conflicts and character arcs executed in this installment and the finale. Elena already had so many reasons to be angry with Blake and push him away. Blake already had many reasons to feel guilty and need to go through a redemption arc.

In fact, Elena has gone through so much trauma, at this point, her assault is just… excessive. It’s going to an extreme because the author already put Elena through so much, there’s not much Elena can experience now that’d up the ante. R*pe should never be used for drama and shock appeal for the audience.

Also, the scene is not fade to black, it is described—not gratuitously, mind you, but it’s enough that readers do need to be mindful going in, because you get the brunt of the experience and are constantly reminded of it because Elena constantly describes nightmares about it. I think the author really should have been content that she’d written enough trauma for her characters and not crossed the line into using r*pe for shock appeal.

Now I can talk about the world building and writing choices, starting with the author having one of the weakest executions of a major conflict and reveal that I’ve ever seen.

Elena, after thinking she had killed Blake and ruined any hope of saving the world, runs away midway through the book and ends up getting past the creepers into Etan, which has become a place of oppression and suffering under Goran’s rule. The author wanted this fact to be a big reveal near the end of the book but decided the only way she could achieve this is by… having Elena refuse to tell anyone any details about where she was when she was missing for several months.

It’s literally framed like everyone begs her again and again to give them any detail that might help and her thinking every single time no, they don’t know anything, they didn’t even know the names of wyverns, there’s no way the names of the towns I was in would do anything, so I just won’t tell them what they are. Which is just… so stupid and makes no sense. Literally the only reason Elena doesn’t tell anyone is because the moment she named one town, every other character would have known immediately where she’d been.

If the author cared that much about delaying the reveal of this information to later in the story so there could be additional conflict and strife as everyone desperately tries to understand what had happened to Elena, then there were better ways to accomplish it. Hell, I would have accepted that Elena was suffering temporary amnesia and it all comes back to her at the opportune moment or that she’s just so traumatized she can’t speak about her experiences whatsoever. Like, they were right there! And this series isn’t afraid of doing something corny or cliché, so I’m baffled that the author landed on Elena deciding the half a second it’d take to say one town name was so pointless she wouldn’t bother.

Next up, we get the weird, weird world building involving the “denting” process the author’s been alluding to for ages now. It gives me Twilight werewolf imprint vibes, I’ll be honest.

In this universe, when dragons who are part of dents go through the “denting process” they are thrown back in time as like, ghostly specters, to when their dent was born and they get to linger around watching them grow up for ~16 years before waking up back in the present, deeply in love with their dent and eager to be with them after years of being able to watch and listen but not talk to or touch. Blake describes is as like “first you love her as a brother, then a protector, then as more” which is pretty creepy, all things considered.

I appreciate that the author wanted to create an air of mystery with the denting process and wanted a solid explanation for why dragons have such a drastic shift in behavior after denting. For a lot of the series, both the reader and Elena are caught between wondering if it’s merely a spell that kicks in, artificially forcing dragons to be in love, or if it’s something more, and it becomes a source of conflict in books 5 and 6. She certainly did justify why dragons would have such deep feelings—upwards of 20 years of watching someone grow up would certainly make anyone have a pretty deep sense of understanding and attachment—it’s just that, the longer you think about the implications, the more uncomfortable this piece of world building gets.

At one point it’s established that Blake is three years older than Elena. That means, when he dents and is thrown back in time to watch Elena grow up, he is about 21. He met Elena when she was about 15 and has, over the course of three years, struggled with both tender and hateful feelings for her—with this in mind, he’s sent back to when Elena is a baby and over the course of the ~15 years goes from developing brotherly feelings for a small child to eventually nursing romantic/sexual feelings for her as a teenager before waking up when she’s 18, absolutely devoted to her and madly in love.

Can’t say if the author didn’t think this was a problem or didn’t think it through enough to realize what it implies, but, given this information, what this means is 21 year old Blake lived 16 years mentally watching Elena grow up and developing romantic feelings for her, so, he comes back mentally aged to 37 while Elena is still just 18 and doesn’t have the same experience of watching Blake grow up for many years. It’s just creepy, this idea of a man watching a child and shifting from brotherly feelings to romantic when she’s “of age.”

Also, yes, there’s a gap of a few years. Blake was sent back to the present the night Elena arrived in Paegia, so his 21-year-old dented self didn’t watch her from that point onward, missing about three years’ worth of ghost stalking. I don’t know what the explanation for that is beyond the author wanting those crucial years to be missing so Blake had to actually work to get to know Elena and develop a relationship with her as she is, not who she used to be. Like, I think that’s nice that the author didn’t want to shortchange the audience of that relationship development, but, I don’t think the author bothered explaining why it happened—for all intents and purposes, it feels like denting would allow them to watch their dent up until the moment they dented.

Also an observation about denting: Blake and George insist they can never explain what denting is because it'd endanger their dents, but like... everyone already knows dents are like magic soulmates and what not, so, if someone wanted to hurt either, they still would probably considering targeting Elena and Becky. The knowledge that they both creepily watched their dents grow up doesn't give potential enemies any useful information. They still are aware dragons care deeply for their dents and therefore if you want to harm a dragon, hurt their dent.

In that same vein, another quality of the author’s writing that bugs me is that she has such a wishy-washy perception of like purity culture and sex, as demonstrated by the way characters all treat Elena now that it’s well-known Blake is her dent.

In Moonbreeze, Elena is 18, but almost all the older adults around her start being weird about her sex life. It’s like… they all start acting like Elena and Blake, being dents, are going to immediately want to smash, but that they should wait for… some reason? I guess the implication is that there’s some lingering Christian purity culture ideals and the proper thing is for them to be married first. Which I find stupid for a number of reasons.

Firstly of all, both are legal adults who can make their own choices about their bodies.
Secondly, neither are virgins by this point, so there’s no “virtue” to protect, which isn’t even something that this universe feels like it’d care about. In book 1, everyone made fun of Elena for being embarrassed by the topic of sex and we know several other characters had premarital sex—like Lucian and Becky. Blake, also, was immediately established as someone who has had lots of sex with lots of partners, and he suffers no real consequences for that.
Thirdly, Blake and Elena are dents—magical soulmates destined to be madly in love and fiercely loyal forever—so what exactly about a marriage vow, besides it just, earning the approval of the Christian god, is needed for these two? It’s not as if Blake is going to seduce Elena into sex, impregnate her, and then abandon her. His commitment to her is absolute.

In this book in particular, we have a strange scene where Blake’s parents, who consider Elena part of the family, kind of take on the role of like… overprotective parents keeping their teen daughter from being alone with boys/having sex, while also having them like, understand that Elena and their son will eventually want access to one another once he dents and they must facilitate it. It’s weird.

So, in their newly built house, Elena has a bedroom. This bedroom is located next to Blake’s parents’ room, has no soundproofing, and has squeaky floorboards—so they can catch him sneaking into her room or hear them moaning while having sex, I suppose. Yet, despite these features clearly meant to keep the two from being alone together, they also built her room with a dragon entrance so that Blake can land outside her bedroom in his dragon form, change into his human form, and enter her bedroom through a door. What’s stopping him from just… using that as a means of getting into her bedroom to fuck her if that’s what they wanted to do? Also, like, when they have all these features built, Blake still hates Elena’s guts and is extremely uninterested in her, so like… they really just anticipated them wanting to have sex in the near future and wanted to try to prevent it for, again, no discernable reason.

On the flip of this, at one point in the story, Blake and Elena are sent off on a quest all alone—a quest that, as far as they know, may take a very long time—so the council and a bunch of adults who seem to not want Elena to have sex with Blake then put her in a position where she has the perfect opportunity to spend hours every day doing just that. Like, again, if keeping the two of them from having sex is of some importance, why were they sent off into the wild all alone? The book is really inconsistent about whether it’s real cool with sex or really not. One moment everyone is begging Elena and Blake to just finally accept that they’re dents and be in love so they can fulfill their destiny, the nex,t everyone is wagging their fingers like “Uh-uh, you two crazy kids can’t sleep in the same bed, you’ll end up doing a sex and we can’t have that.”

It's always baffled me how fast and loose the author is with how she approaches her characters and sex. I don’t understand what this wishy-washy approach to sex and purity culture is accomplishing for the narrative—in this installment, outside of Blake kind of forlornly thinking about how much he wants to be with Elena and how upset his is at himself for making her hate him this much, the two of them aren’t even trying to have sex with each other. It’s all projected onto them by the adults who are just anticipating what they’ll be like eventually and not explaining why that’s a problem.

As I mentioned in my Frostbite review, since I've mentioned it so heavily, I won't harp on the author's continued errors and strange phrasing, but, just know it's still present in Moonbreeze. It's gotten a bit better, but, not enough to not be noticeable still.

Oh, one observation I made that I want to mention. In previous books, the author like, danced around using the word fuck. Like she'd just say "mother" instead of "mother fucker" but now has apparently decided that although Elena shouldn't have sex with Blake, it's totally fine for everyone to say the word fuck.

Not sure how to quite wrap this review up beyond just saying I liked it but it did have a few pretty oof things about it. Might return later to use up my remaining 3300 characters to talk about some other things if I remember them, but I've already been waffling on this review for a few weeks, so I just want to get this up at long last.
