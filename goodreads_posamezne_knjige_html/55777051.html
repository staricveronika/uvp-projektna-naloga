Welcome to Horn-Horn! A small town in New York State, fully equipped with oddball residents, secrets around every corner, and more than one illegal alien (literally) lurking in the haunted woods... If you loved the style of Beetlejuice, Northern Exposure, Goosebumps, The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy and Mean Girls, you will fall in love with Horn-Horn!

When teenage loner Cassie Gellar moves with her family to the seaside town of Horn-Horn, she finds herself at odds with the battling cliques at her new school.

There’s Eleanore Parker, Principal’s daughter and social butterfly. Then there’s Hayley and the ‘backseater’ misfits.

But there’s also Zag, a shackled little boy she finds hiding in the woods. Where is he from, and why does he have a woman on a moose chasing after him?

Cassie finds herself catapulted into a world of magic and danger, one where nothing is ever as it seems and nobody is to be trusted.

Full of intelligent comedy, fantasy and horror, the constantly evolving town of Horn-Horn proves that friendship and family can transcend even space and time itself.

A stand-up comedy about a frustrated teen and a wishing child.

Sixteen-year-old Cassie is new in the town of Horn-Horn and seeks to fit in at the cliquish high school, shunning the most popular clique for the least popular. Even so, her friendships are tenuous and shaky.

The story reads like stand-up comedy, about the sadness of a teenager's world, the cliques, the poor self-image, the negative outlooks, and wanting to fit in. The teens are portrayed as frustrated, shallow, and mean. The adults aren't much better. All are odd, along with the town and its history. Oddness can be charming if it isn't mean or dangerous, but everything seems weird in non-endearing ways.

I thought this was a coming of age novel, but a surprise fantasy enters about 77 pages in. Cassie finds herself hiding an alien boy who lives in a clay head. A bit further in, we enter what appears to be a separate novel. We leave Cassie's first person point of view to enter Ursula's third person viewpoint. She is princess of the planet Danube, and in a fit of rage she kicks her wishing child into the unknown. This is the boy in the clay head. Regretting her action, she searches for him on Earth. That brings us back to Cassie's point of view. From now on, we will enter other viewpoints, some of which are slippery.

Actually the slips didn't bother me. What jolted was the first viewpoint shift about 100 pages into the novel. And the sudden fantasy. The late introduction gives a disjointed feel to the story. The author should show near the beginning that the story is a fantasy. Also she needs to prepare the reader for shifts in viewpoint. Another element of confusion is that the book is written in British English, along with made-up slang, though the story is set in the USA. This includes odd phrasing and word choices, so I don't know what is correct and what isn't.

I was already bothered by the cruelty, even if comic. However, characters do evolve, so we find touches of compassion and understanding. That's when I began to like certain individuals. I like Tanya because she is kind from the beginning, even though she is a minor character. I like Zag, the wishing child, and I am glad Cassie agrees to take care of him.

I liked the story when compassion entered in and when Cassie's domestic and foreign problems escalated. The tension mounts over the fight for the wishing child, even as Cassie continues her struggles to fit in. And then there are the amazing odd twists.

For me, this rollicking story is too energetic and too long. Others may guffaw and eagerly anticipate more antics. Certainly the novel ends on a satisfying note that leaves an opening for a sequel.

    coming-of-age fantasy fiction

Got to be the most original book out there.

....

And holy hell is it a big giant mesh of genres.

....

In saying that, it does have its flaws. But most books do.

....


Pros:

The style of writing is biting and dramatic in all the right places. The prose is absolutely beautiful. Is the narrator meant to be a poet?

The characters seem to be developed well. Those that are not often give random comments or there are suggestions of future revelations to come.

The twists are insane. Insane, I tell you. They just keep coming and they get crazier with each one. I found myself sometimes sitting up laughing at what was happening - because I was loving where it was going. Madness.

Brendan Gellar. I hope he is a more prominent character in the next one because he's the one with the most potential. Mentioned he's gay at 11 once and never mentioned again... brilliant.

Hardly any mention of current technology, which I like, as it means it will hold up in a decade. Also stories are too reliant on these forms of communications in modern books. It gives me a gross feeling.

The secret about the planets. That's some great fantasy writing right there.

The references. So many references. I'm a child of the late eighties and I caught some things that would go over the heads of many actual teenagers out there.

The contemporary side to things. It's not quite like Twin Peaks, not quite like Mean Girls, but it's somewhere in the middle. A mix of Freaks & Geeks and Simon vs. The Homo Sapien Agenda.

The absolute lack of regard for sensitive PC whingers is a breath of fresh air in this current climate where everything has to be spelt with eggshells. There are lines like 'I thought she was going to go full lesbian on me', 'Oh go and sit on your washing machine' and a hilarious bash at Scientology that could make both Republicans, Liberals and Scientologists squirm... But bring it on, I say. There's this very small iota of society that are still human. This is gold to us.

....

Cons:

The third person narrative: Only because it's so sudden and out there. But as per the note at the beginning, it'll be explained in a later story. So with the note I was prepared. But still... For the first little while it was bothersome. Is it still Cassie narrating? Even though she's not there. Stranger things have happened.

The ending is a downer. That's all I'm going to say. Glad there's more to come.

There are SO many mysteries left unanswered. I suppose one day when there are like 4 other books or so it won't seem like a problem but now, where we are with the series, it's a problem for me. I will have to re-read it again before the sequel to refresh my memories about all of them.

I'm bothered by the overdone nature of the 'Saviours'. Perhaps a little too sitcom-y? I guess some teen girls are that dramatic in real life.

Location: Okay so it's not in the USA, it's not in the UK, Australia, France... Where the hell IS Horn-Horn??

....

Other than these few problems it's a near perfect read. Can we put this book as a must-read already?

Because it was so unexpected, it was actually very alarming to me, which I guess was the intention.

So the magic part is indeed a HUGE part to this story, but really at the heart of it all is the very weird town itself, a name as strange as its town. It seems to have more mysteries going on than a Sherlock Holmes Omnibus, and I can only pray there is at least one more sequel to address these mysteries because I am thirsty for more.

The most appealing thing to me is Cassie's narration. She's a great lead and a joy to experience. The strangeness she encounters throughout the book is handled with such dry wit, you'd half expect her to grow up to be a stand-up comedian. I can't count the number of times I laughed out loud reading this book. Maybe don't attempt it on a bus/in public.

I have a feeling this is going to be one of those books in the near future that will explode purely from word of mouth. It's self-published despite its professionalism, and at the end of the day it doesn't matter because I am sure that one day it WON'T BE. This is gonna blow up for sure. Give it a movie, a franchise, whatever. As long as there's a sequel.

Not since Lewis Carroll has there been such an unusual story unleashed upon the world.

I was given a proofread of this before it was released. Given my track record with young adult novels I was very excited to see what the heck this was about (the title gives away nothing).

I was not disappointed.
Like, AT ALL.

The story revolves around Cassie Gellar and her family's move to the town of, you guessed it, Horn-Horn, which is really just a contemporary name for Two Horn (which the Native Indians called it in the 1800's). The town from the get-go is utterly bizarre. Its local cemetery shares its venue with the local art gallery, for instance, and there's a teacher with about a million garden gnome statuettes, and there's this very narrow-minded clique in the school of four popular girls who call themselves 'The Saviours of Tomorrow'.

While Cassie settles into the social world of high school, which is probably the first and foremost part to this entire thing, she eventually (and quite unexpectedly) stumbles upon a little boy in the local woods who is hiding from a giant rabid moose. I couldn't even begin to explain what happens next. It suddenly propels us as a reader into a magical sub-plot that eventually seeps right into the very core of the storyline.

As a whole this book is DEEPLY intriguing. It's obvious that the author knows her way with words, and the narration-style is very attractive. There's much to love in the townsfolk, which harks back to the quirky ways of Stars Hollow, Three Billboards Outside Ebbing, Missouri; Everwood even, and any other particularly side-character driven piece one might think of. It's all rather charming. Even the characters from another world seem to be in on the whole gag - nobody is particularly normal and that, in itself, seems to be the norm within the Horn-Horn universe.

There are so many twists and turns here that it is at times mind-bending. It's definitely a book you will have to go back and read again, not because of getting lost, but because you'll want to pick up on things that weren't obvious the first time around. The twists. Just. Keep. Coming. And none of them suffer the usual gimmicky route - they're all deeply rooted in the story.

Fans of anything from To Kill A Mockingbird to Hitchhiker's Guide will love this book, and I hear it's the first in a series, which soothes my curious heart for the subjects left unanswered. It's a very British humour that runs through the veins of Horn-Horn's soul and, despite being apparently set in an alternate United States, there are British and even Australian colloquialisms throughout that make me question what territory we're reading about.

Do give it a try. With sequels I'm sure it will be even more impossible to put down. And of course, my final question to the author is: What were you smoking?

“It was as if a rock had fallen from a nearby cliff and landed on a see-saw and then flung a kid into a bike stack and the last bike to fall had landed on a dog’s tail and the dog had been tied to a gazebo and as the dog ran off the gazebo’s support was broken off and the gazebo had fallen down and revealed a man and a woman having intimate relations inside. Quickly this mundane story takes a turn for the “unusual” when Cassie gets attacked by a moose and discovers a young boy (Zag) with a clay head in the forested area. Unbeknownst to Cassie - Zag’s previous owner is looking for Zag.

A story as strange as its name...

‘She looked like she would rather eat twenty pregnant slugs, spew them up and eat them again as the babies wriggled down her throat.’

The Gellar family are rich and have moved to a huge house (imported from France) in the coastal town of Horn-Horn. Cassie Gellar is sixteen and starts at the local school the unwilling prize of Eleanore Parker, one of the ‘in crowd’ girls, so she deliberately attaches herself to Hayley Gauche, one of the social outcasts, the ‘back-seaters’. This leads to some interesting social tensions. The town of Horn-Horn itself is a little strange and then there are those creepy woods…

Then there is Ursula the unaging princess who lives is given a birthday present by her father the King - a stone head with an orphan called Zag inside who can grant wishes...

Horn-Horn is an odd book, it is definitely YA and the chatty style of Cassie makes her an easy companion.

‘The two demented moons were nearly passing overhead, and their gentle mingling light gave her enough source to discover that she was about sixty feet from the tiny cabin she had intended to arrive inside.’

It’s greatest strength is in its quirkiness, which I strongly suspect a reader will either love or hate. The mix of science fantasy and a bizarre ‘Twin-Peaks’ like reality I found very engaging. Cassies marvellously funny and often grotesque observations out of left-field are a real delight.The story is very strange and the way it is told is stranger. The writing is easy to read, but the constructions and language use are often quite bizarre. The story is well paced, aside a few dips into back-story, but those are handled in an often humorous manner so they don’t seem to weigh too heavily. The characters are not really deep, but are all well painted.

‘I watched from my place on the ground as Dad was lifted into the sky as fast as a cannon and disappeared into the clouds above.’

The biggest issue I have with the book - and for me it was a big one, but some people might not find it so, was the slip between first and third person. It creates a very uncomfortable and jarring immersion breaker each time it happens and completely breaks up the flow of the story. When the transition happens with no warning - even from one paragraph to the next - it is disorienting. In my view it would have been much better if the story had been told in third person throughout, then the whole would have flowed more smoothly. The other issue I had was with the end, for me it did not work, but I can’t go into too much on that as it would be a major spoiler.

This is a YA fantasia for those who are looking for something totally unique and who enjoy books where fantasy and reality collide full on in an explosion of stars!

Wonderful premise for a story.

There are two different story lines in this book that collide. When I first began reading it, I fell in love. The first-person narrative at the beginning of the book comes from the sardonic and witty voice of a school aged young woman who is the voice of reason in her family of wealthy hippie parents and a rebelling slightly self-destructive brother. She finds herself at a new school and has to negotiate the -friend trap between the Cordelia’s of the school (the in crowd) and the slightly cooler Xanders (the cast -aways). As she struggles with the predictable pitfalls made more fun by her biting commentary, she stumbles upon an otherworldly creature trying to survive in the forest.

The problem begins when we switch to the third person narrative, which follows a spoiled magic welder who would be Jasmine if we continue the Buffy analogy. There is just nothing to like about this character and the sections that focus on her are weighed down with backstory and trivial one dimensional support characters.

Eventually, the two plot lines do come together as both parties face off in their efforts to find and keep the lost soul from the forest, however the two plots feel to be at odds with each other because the narrative that follows the teenager is rich in teen-life details and the leg the follows the adult is likewise fleshed out with adult themed wicked-witch priorities.

I think there is a good book in here with fun characters and a nice adventure. Perhaps a younger reader would not get as bogged down in the minutia of the world building and would enjoy it more.

Power games can manifest in many forms - and the story of Horn-Horn has lead character Cassie Gellar caught in the middle of them.
Whether it's the power games going on in the cliques of her new school, or those being played by the supernatural forces from the world of Danube, it's hard to tell which is the more perilous for Cassie.
Horn-Horn is the name of the town that Cassie and her family have moved to, and just as she's starting to get to grips with the oddness of the town's residents, she discovers Zag, a mysterious child with the power to grant wishes, stranded in the nearby woods. She takes him in, only to find he is being pursued by Ursula, a dangerous being from Danube - which seems part a distant world in our solar system and part an almost faerie court. There's a bit of perspective hopping along the way - Cassie's tale is told in first person, while others are told in third person, which is a bit jarring.
As a YA tale, it's great in terms of the details of teenage life that it paints - the cruelties, the eagerness to find friends, the delights and the loneliness. This is both a strength and a bit of a flaw, in that the book spends such time on this aspect that the main plot itself takes some time to get going. I'm not sure I'd want the snark and the details of teenage life to be slimmed down, though, because they're the best thing about this book.
That, and its quirkiness, make me give this book a thumbs-up, even if it feels as if the otherworldly aspects don't quite make their mark.

The book itself features a whole double-sided fly leaf of praise from the Internet, of which, dear reader, I will happily liquify with my laser moose eyes of disdain.

First of all, this book is deathly boring and I put it down dozens of times during the day I read it, just absolutely sick of its shit. I hit twenty percent— ok, a slow burner. Thirtyfourty— Fifty percent! nothing had happened. I put it down to call my mom to tell her my book wasn’t very good. Seventy five percent, STILL nothing happened. I actually kept bookmarking, figured out the percentage of the portion I’d had read, and thought SEVENTY EIGHT PERCENT AND NOTHING HAS HAPPENED. Nothing happened until the last 20% of the book.

It has a short prechapter wacky Viking Alien Princess prologue, and then it’s hundreds of pages of high school Mean Girls and clique politics. When the first magic thing happens, it doesn’t actually change the trajectory of the story in any way until the denouement happens.

The final 20% is, actually, good! But it took so much effort to get to that point that I don’t blame people who DNF 10 pages in AT ALL.

Yes. When stuff happens, it is wacky. And there is a lot of wacky stuffed in here. But it truly is a Book About Teenagers in High School…with some parallel magic. It is kind of a comedy novel, in its sheer absurdity sometimes, but I wouldn’t call it comedic as if you’re laughing at any point. In the very beginning the father has to stop the family bus to have diarrhoea a lot because he has worms from “letting a dog lick his face.” Woof.

Another thing— I wouldn’t call this a YA novel. Just a few pages in, the sheer sexualization of Ursula made me think “Oh, I can’t give this to my teenage nieces because it’s kind of vulgar” and it hits that note A LOT. It also contains antisemitism, racism, and the words pussy and retard. Perhaps the racism was excusable because it was for characterization, but for a book written in 2017 the word “retard” is unacceptable. It was used in an exposition part, and not as a “a character is saying this and I know you know that we both know this is unacceptable” but as the actual term for mental disability. The racism was, on the other hand, used as signifiers for shitbird characters.

The claims say this book gives “Beetlejuice vibes”—perhaps it was meant to mean the type of humour and absurdism? I’ll give them that (only just) but this doesn’t share genre or any discernable narrative similarities to Beetlejuice.

To the reviewer who claimed this was a “major successor to The Hunger Games,” respectfully: what the ⨍υׁׅᝯׁƙׁׅ֑? This is not dystopian speculative science fiction, and again, this shares no discernable narrative similarities to The Hunger Games. Not vibe, not humour, not dialogue, not themes. I’m just absolutely mystified by this assertion and worry the reviewer might have read another book than I did.

All in all, I enjoyed the last 80 pages, and I was really begging the book to keep me interested for the rest of the adventure. There are some interesting narrative choices, and a lot of things won’t add up throughout when it comes to anachronisms and supposed timelines, which frustrated me in reading, mostly because I was a teen when this is supposedly taking place, but everything was off. It eventually made sense, but it was alienating. There is a really ham fisted part in the action portion, and I wondered if the whole thing was set in 2001 just to shoehorn it in. I didn’t like that. If you read it, you’ll know what I mean.

I could have lived without reading this book, honestly. Creativity is nice, but I—- well, you know. I think the most disappointing books recently have been ones that have a whole laundry list of disparate ideas that are just forcibly melted and steamrolled together. The Thousand Deaths of Ardor Benn, The Eyre Affair, and this one. That’s what they all have in common: I sought out books for time travel, and ended up running into this distinct liquorice allsorts genre.

I digress.

I've long been an avid avoider of the comical in novels. For the longest time I thought they didn't belong. Here and there most stories had some humor to them but it was never classed as a 'comical' read.
Horn-Horn changes that, as the genre is firmly set in the absurd. When I first started reading this I was in love with the narration, and somewhere towards the first third it slips to third-person which threw me off at first. But as the story goes on it becomes more and more engaging, you don't notice in the end.
The storyline regards Cassie, a teen who moves to the seaside town with her parents and siblings, and has trouble fitting in to the social hierarchy at her new school (aptly named 'Horn-Horn High-High'). This is what you expect, reading on, to be the main plot of the story, but a random bit of fantasy enters the story and from then-on it's a fine balance between comical high school misadventures, and paranormal dilemmas. It all works out though, and by the end you have so many questions left unanswered it's got you captured in its spell.
I'd recommend for people who love the rebellious in nature, and who love their modern stories doused in an array of intense, sometimes scary, supernatural.

4/5
